The Stroke-Adding Rule

Letters shaped like the sounds they make

Hangul's five basic consonants are literal sketches of your mouth and throat mid-sound. Add one stroke for a stronger sound — that's the whole rulebook. Nothing to memorize, everything to derive.

Heaven, Earth, Human

Three strokes, infinite vowels

Every vowel starts from just three shapes: the roundness of the sky, the flatness of the earth, a person standing tall. Combine them and you get every vowel below.

Live Now

First up: Japanese

Type on your regular Dubeolsik keyboard, get real-time Hiragana. A statistical engine trained on real usage fills in the idioms no plain rulebook could ever catch.

  • Real-time conversion — Dubeolsik in, Hiragana out, no retraining needed
  • Statistically trained — learned from 110k Japanese Wikipedia sentences to catch what rules alone miss
  • Context reranking — reads the whole sentence to pick the most natural candidate
  • Built for macOS — native InputMethodKit app, ready today. Windows is next.

One Script, Every Language

What's next?

Hangul has already proven it can go beyond Korean. In 2009, the Cia-Cia language of Buton Island, Indonesia — with no writing system of its own — adopted Hangul because it captured their sounds best. Hangulpia turns that experiment into a product.
